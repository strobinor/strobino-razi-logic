# Fakhr ad-Dīn ar-Rāzī's Logic

## Introduction

Ibn Khaldūn's *Muqaddima* preserves a passage of remarkable historiographical interest. After observing that the study of logic had been transformed beyond recognition since its Greek and early Arabic beginnings, the historian identifies a decisive turning point: "Treatment of [logic] has become lengthy and wide-ranging—the first to do this was Fakhr ad-Dīn ar-Rāzī [(d. 1210)] and, after him, Afḍal al-Dīn al-Khūnajī [(d. 1248)], on whom Eastern scholars rely even now." The verdict is unequivocal: "The books and ways of the ancients have been abandoned, as though they had never been."[1]

This portrait of Rāzī's pivotal position—standing between Avicenna (d. 1037) and the post-Avicennan tradition that would dominate Arabic-Islamic intellectual history for centuries—orients the present inquiry. The history of Arabic logic divides, as a matter of scholarly consensus, into a pre-Avicennan and a post-Avicennan phase.[2] The former was characterized by a relatively direct engagement with Aristotle's *Organon*, mediated through translation and commentary; the latter, inaugurated by Avicenna's two major methods of presentation of logic—the traditional account in the *Shifāʾ* (and cognate works like the *Najāt*) and the innovative, compressed and rearranged framework of the *Ishārāt wa-t-tanbīhāt* (somewhat captured by the extant portion of *al-Mashriqiyyūn*)—set the terms for virtually all subsequent work in the discipline. Rāzī stands at the cardinal point between these two epochs: the first major thinker to take up Avicenna's invitation to rethink the foundations, and the figure whose formulations would become standard equipment for later logicians, in terms of conceptual vocabulary and research agenda.

The present chapter pursues a dual objective. The first is to provide an accessible introduction to Rāzī's logical project, situating his work within the broader trajectory of Arabic logic and offering a sense of its scope and ambitions. The second is to supply what one might call precise coordinates for the localization of signature views and doctrines in Rāzī's most influential logical work, the *Manṭiq al-Mulakhkhaṣ*. This is, in effect, an exercise in cartography: mapping a large and sophisticated treatise so that its contents become navigable for historians of philosophy and logic who may approach the text from different directions and with different interests.

The chapter is organized in two main sections. Section I situates Rāzī's approach to logic, briefly examines the matrix of his logical works, and identifies the distinctive character of the *Mulakhkhaṣ* within the landscape of post-Avicennan logic. Section II offers a guided tour of the work's contents, following its internal division into conceptions and assertions, and locating the signature doctrines in their proper place within the system. It is in this second section that the essentialist and the externalist reading of the subject term (*ḥaqīqī*/*khārijī*), the referential/descriptional distinction (*dhātī*/*waṣfī*), the analysis of hypothetical propositions and their combinatorial complexity, the canonical list of thirteen categorical propositions, the categorical and hypothetical syllogistic, and the eclipse of demonstration are presented and discussed.

## I Rāzī's Approach to Logic and the Place of the *Mulakhkhaṣ*

### Rāzī in the Development of Arabic Logic

Fakhr ad-Dīn ar-Rāzī is a figure of extraordinary scope. Primarily known as a theologian and exegete, he is also one of the most consequential logicians in the Arabic tradition.[3] His significance for logic lies not in a single revolutionary contribution but in a sustained effort of systematization, refinement, and—in certain areas—genuine innovation. What Avicenna did to ancient logic, Rāzī does, on a smaller but nonetheless consequential scale, to Avicenna's: he reorganizes, expands, contracts, revises, and crystallizes its shape and content. This "Avicenna transformed" (in Rāzī's hands) becomes in essence the new canon of logic in Arabic.

The post-Avicennan tradition in logic has been accurately characterized by the distinction between two intellectual temperaments: revisionists, who engaged critically and sometimes radically with Avicenna's framework, and purists, who sought to defend the master's original positions.[4] Rāzī belongs firmly in the revisionist camp. His engagement with Avicenna is neither servile nor gratuitously polemical; it is the work of a thinker who has fully absorbed the Avicennan system and proceeds to subject it to critical scrutiny, reorganization, and selective correction. To understand the nature of this transformation, it is helpful to consider its structural dimensions. The pre-Avicennan trajectory of Arabic logic was determined by Aristotle's extended *Organon* (Porphyry's *Isagoge*, the eight works of the *Organon* proper, followed by the *Rhetoric* and the *Poetics*) and by the Greek commentary tradition. With Avicenna, an important transformation began: much of the logical corpus still followed the traditional order of presentation and the traditional agenda of problems, but the foundations were being remodelled—most dramatically in the *Qiyās* of the *Shifāʾ*, where between a third and a half of the material has no precedent in Aristotle and represents Avicenna at his most original. It is this innovative material—above all, the recasting of the theory of propositions, a conceptually different understanding of the basis for the modal syllogistic, and the full-blown logic of hypotheticals,—that gets picked up by the later tradition, beginning with Rāzī. If the *Shifāʾ* contains the raw materials of the transformation, the *Ishārāt* is the strain that would survive, mutate, and spread: the history of Arabic logic after Avicenna is, for most intents and purposes, an exercise in tracking how this variant evolves.[5]

The result of Rāzī's engagement is a body of logical doctrine that proved enormously influential: the technical vocabulary stabilized by Rāzī in the *Mulakhkhaṣ* would become the standard idiom of Arabic logic for centuries, transmitted through handbooks such as Kātibī's *Shamsiyya* and elaborated by generations of commentators and super-commentators.[6]

To appreciate the magnitude of his influence, one might consider an (admittedly imperfect) analogy with Alexander of Aphrodisias and his role in the transformation of Aristotle—an authoritative interpreter whose critical engagement with the master's works sets the agenda for  subsequent discussions. Rāzī stands in a somewhat similar relation to Avicenna, with the further qualification that Avicenna's corpus, unlike Aristotle's, was largely intentional in the form in which it came down to posterity and under significant authorial control. The shift from the Aristotelian *Organon* to the Avicennan restructuring of logic is, in important respects, a shift in the very identity of the discipline; and it is Rāzī who, more than any other single figure, consolidates and propagates the new identity.[7]

### Rāzī's Logical Works

Rāzī's logical output can be organized along two axes: the distinction between exegetical and independent works, and the question of authenticity. Among the exegetical works, three deserve mention. The earliest is the *Lubāb al-Ishārāt wa-t-tanbīhāt*, a compendium of Avicenna's last summa. The *Sharḥ al-Ishārāt wa-t-tanbīhāt*, a full-length commentary, represents a more mature and more critical engagement with the same text; it was the first major commentary on the *Ishārāt* and set the standard for the genre.[8] The *Sharḥ ʿUyūn al-ḥikma*, a commentary on Avicenna's shorter *ʿUyūn al-ḥikma*, is likely the last of Rāzī's genuine logical works; it contains cross-references to the *Mulakhkhaṣ*, supporting a later dating and suggesting that Rāzī regarded the *Mulakhkhaṣ* as the authoritative statement of his logical views.[9]

The main independent systematic work is, therefore, the *Manṭiq al-Mulakhkhaṣ*, which stands as Rāzī's most comprehensive and original treatment of logic. It is worth noting, however, that the corpus of Rāzī's "logical works" in a strict sense does not exhaust the places where his logic is developed and deployed. In works that fall outside this corpus—most notably the *Nihāyat al-ʿuqūl* and the *Mabāḥith al-mashriqiyya*—logical tools and arguments are put to work in the service of broader philosophical and theological purposes. This chapter does not address these connections, but the relationship between Rāzī's use of logic in applied contexts and the formal presentation of it in the dedicated logical works remains an important question for future research.[10]

The relationship between the exegetical and the independent works is instructive. In the commentaries, Rāzī's agenda is constrained by the structure and content of the base text; his critical observations, however penetrating, remain tethered to Avicenna's order of exposition. In the *Mulakhkhaṣ*, by contrast, this connection is much looser, and Rāzī is free to pursue his own organizational instincts: it is here that his distinctive contributions emerge most clearly. The work is less indebted to the letter of Avicenna than any of the commentaries, and it displays the kind of systematic ambition that comes from a thinker who has worked through the tradition and is now prepared to recast it on his own terms. 

### Why the *Mulakhkhaṣ*?

The *Mulakhkhaṣ* is the natural focus of the present chapter for the reasons outlined above, with the added consideration that its authorship has never been seriously questioned. In its prologue, the work is presented as a "summary of the investigations of the earlier scholars (*mulakhkhaṣ abḥāth al-mutaqaddimīn*) and a collection of the conclusions of the opinions of the first authorities, together with some valuable additions of our own reflection" (*Mulakhkhaṣ*,Premise, p. 7). The earlier scholars are, at this stage, Rāzī's predecessors in the Arabic logical tradition—al-Fārābī, Avicenna, Sāwī, Abū l-Barakāt al-Baghdādī—no longer Aristotle and the Greek commentary tradition. The leading principle, Rāzī announces, will be the criterion of demonstration: if a view is demonstrably true, he will side with tradition; if conflicting views arise that appear equally plausible, the mind must independently exercise its own critical judgment.

The *Mulakhkhaṣ* is a large and densely organized treatise (spanning over 340 pages in the 2002 edition) of tightly argued material. Its internal organization is governed by a hierarchical system of structural divisions—e.g., *jumla* (major division), *bāb* (section), *qism* (part), *mabḥath* (investigation), and *baḥth* (discussion)—that reflects Rāzī's characteristically meticulous approach to the architecture of a treatise. An interactive digital synopsis of the complete content of the work has been prepared as a companion to the present chapter, offering a navigable overview of the text down to individual discussions and sub-discussions.[11] What follows is necessarily selective: the aim is not exhaustive coverage but a focused guide to those doctrinal points that are most salient for the student of post-Avicennan Arabic logic.

### Distinctive Contributions at a Glance

Before entering into the details of the *Mulakhkhaṣ*, it is useful to identify, in broad strokes, the main areas in which Rāzī's logical work is most distinctive. These may be grouped under four headings. First, organization: Rāzī adopts, enhances, and crystallizes the presentation of logic found in Avicenna's *Ishārāt*, imposing a level of systematic order that was not present—or not consistently present—in any prior text. Second, expansion: Rāzī adds material that has no precedent in Avicenna, the most conspicuous example being his recognition and systematic treatment of the fourth syllogistic figure. Third, contraction: certain topics that receive extensive treatment in Avicenna—most notably, the theory of demonstration (*burhān*)—are dramatically curtailed in the *Mulakhkhaṣ*, reduced to a brief coda that raises questions about Rāzī's view of not just of the discipline's internal priorities but of its very nature. Fourth, lasting formulations: several technical distinctions and classificatory frameworks that were articulated or firmly established first by Rāzī would become canonical in the later tradition. These include the distinction between the essentialist and the externalist reading of the subject term (*ḥaqīqī*/*khārijī*), the referential and the descriptional reading (*dhātī*/*waṣfī*) of categorical propositions, the resulting canonical list of thirteen types of categorical propositions, with their implications for syllogistic inference.

A characteristic trait that pervades all four areas is what might be called a fondness for enumeration. Rāzī is a systematic classifier: he counts, subdivides, and tabulates with a thoroughness that sometimes borders on the obsessive—and that, precisely for this reason, proved to be of enormous pedagogical and scholarly utility for the later tradition.

## II The Logic of the *Mulakhkhaṣ*

The *Mulakhkhaṣ* opens with a brief introduction (*muqaddima*) addressing two preliminary questions: the need for logic (Premise, pp. 7–9) and the subject matter of logic (Premise, pp. 9–10). Logic is a science that deals with secondary intelligibles insofar as those are required to reason correctly and avoid error in the acquisition of what is unknown from what is known. The fundamental structural division that governs the entire work follows from this assumption and the fact that objects of knowledge are either concepts or propositions. The *Mulakhkhaṣ* divides logic into the study of conception (*taṣawwur*) and assertion (*taṣdīq*).[12] The first major division (*jumla*) addresses how conceptions are acquired; the second addresses how assertions are acquired. This binary architecture, inherited from Avicenna but given definitive form by Rāzī, is the backbone of the work. Each branch has in turn two parts: premises and goals. In the first branch, the premises are, broadly speaking, the predicables and the theoretical machinery required to discuss them; the goals are definitions and descriptions. In the second branch, the premises are propositions, their taxonomy and truth conditions; the goals are productive inferences, their components and logical structure. The present chapter focuses primarily on the second branch and its internal divisions, which is where Rāzī is at his most original and where his innovations prove most consequential for the later tradition.[13]

### Conceptions: Predicables, Definition, and Description

The first *jumla* (1, pp. 11–119) is divided into premises (*muqaddimāt*, 1.1, pp. 13–98) and goals (*maqāṣid*, 1.2, pp. 99–119). The premises provide the conceptual apparatus needed for the treatment of definitions and descriptions. This includes sixteen investigations (*mabāḥith*) distributed across several broad topics: signification and the distinction between correspondence, containment, and implication (1.1.2, pp. 15–21); the simple and the compound (1.1.3, pp. 22–24); universal and particular (1.1.4, pp. 25–34)—itself subdivided into eleven subsidiary investigations; subject and the predicate (1.1.5, pp. 34–35); quiddity and its parts (1.1.6–7, pp. 36–49); external implicates (1.1.8, pp. 50–57); the five predicables individually—genus (1.1.9, pp. 58–65), species (1.1.10, pp. 66–69), the features shared by genus and species (1.1.11, pp. 70–72), differentia (1.1.12, pp. 73–84), proprium (1.1.13, pp. 85–87), and common accident (1.1.84, pp. 87–88)—followed by a discussion of how the five predicables are "hunted" (1.1.15, pp. 89–91) and of their shared characteristics (1.1.16, pp. 92–98).[14]

Several features of this first *jumla* deserve attention. The treatment of the predicables is notably extensive and systematic: Rāzī devotes separate investigations to each of the five, preceded by preparatory discussions of the concepts they presuppose. The investigation of the differentia alone spans sixteen sections, reflecting a level of analytical detail that exceeds anything comparable in Avicenna's *Ishārāt*.

The section on goals (1.2, pp. 99–119) treats definition (*ḥadd*) and description (*rasm*) directly. The discussion opens with a division of definitions (*taʿrīfāt*, 1.2.1, pp. 101–108), including a treatment of doubts on the division and their resolution—again, in a dialectical format that is characteristic of Rāzī's method. This is followed by a division of quiddities with respect to definition (1.2.2, pp. 107–8), a discussion of whether definition can be acquired by proof (1.2.6, p. 112), an investigation of the mutual relationship (*munāsaba*) between definitions and descriptions (1.2.7, pp. 113–114), and a treatment of the difficulties in the composition of definitions (1.1.8-9, pp. 115–118). The latter topic receives more care from Rāzī than from most of his predecessors; an interesting reference to Abū l-Barakāt al-Baghdādī appears in this context (1.2.9, p. 118), signalling Rāzī's awareness of alternative approaches to the problem.

### Assertions: Types of Propositions

The second *jumla* (2, pp. 121–355) is the heart of the *Mulakhkhaṣ* and the part of it where Rāzī's distinctive contributions emerge more clearly. It is structured in three main sections (*abwāb*): the valuations (*aḥkām*) of propositions (2.1, pp. 121–237), the syllogistic (*qiyās*) (2.2, pp. 239–340), and demonstration (2.3, pp. 341–355). The treatment of propositions opens with a preliminary section (*muqaddima*) offering a definition of the proposition and a division of propositions into categorical and hypothetical (2.1, (i)-(ii), pp. 123–126).[15]

**Categorical propositions.** The analysis of categorical propositions (2.1.1, pp. 129–204) proceeds through a sequence of increasingly refined investigations. After a treatment of the formal elements of categorical propositions—the role of subject and predicate, copula, quantifiers, affirmation and negation, modality, and the distinction between determinate and indefinite terms (2.1.1.1.1, pp. 129–139)—Rāzī considers subject and predicate as the matter of categorical propositions, and arrives here at two distinctions that are among his most consequential contributions.[16]

*The essentialist and the externalist reading.* The first is the distinction between the essentialist (*ḥaqīqī*) and the externalist (*khārijī*) reading of the subject term of the (universal affirmative) categorical proposition (2.1.1.2.2.1.1.1, pp. 140–143).[17] The problem is this: when we say "Every J is B," what is the scope of the universal quantifier? Two answers are possible according to Rāzī. On the essentialist reading, "every J" ranges over all things that are A in virtue of their essence or quiddity, regardless of whether any actual Js exist in extra-mental reality. The key is a counterfactual clause: were something to exist as J, it would be B. On the externalist reading, by contrast, "every J" ranges only over those things that actually exist externally as Js.

The distinction is illustrated with two canonical examples. Consider "Every triangle is a figure." If there are no actual triangles in extra-mental existence, this is true on the essentialist reading (since whatever would be a triangle, were it to exist, would be a figure) but false on the externalist reading (affirmative propositions with empty subject terms are false in this framework). Conversely, consider "Every figure is a triangle." If the only actual figures in extra-mental existence happen to be triangles, this is true on the externalist reading but false on the essentialist reading. Rāzī articulates the essentialist reading with characteristic precision:

> We do not mean by it what is described as J externally, but rather something more general, which is: were it to exist externally it would be true of it that it is J, whether it exists externally or not. For we can say "every triangle is a figure" even if there are no triangles existent externally. The meaning is rather that everything which would be a triangle were it to exist would be—in so far as it existed—a figure. (Rāzī, *Mulakhkhaṣ* [2002] p. 141; transl. Street)

On the externalist reading, he writes:

> By the second reading, we mean by "every J" every single thing which exists externally among the individual Js… On this hypothesis, were there no heptagons existent externally, it wouldn't be correct to say "every heptagon is a figure"; if the only figures existent externally were triangles, it would be correct to say "every figure is a triangle." On the first reading, both of these would be false. (Rāzī, *Mulakhkhaṣ* [2002] pp. 142–3; transl. Street)

The distinction has direct implications for Rāzī's understanding of basic logical relationships and operations such as contradiction, conversion and contraposition, and consequently for his theory of syllogistic inference. One way to think about the genesis of the distinction is as a way to work out which inferences are licensed under which reading, and which ones are not. But the significance of this distinction extends well beyond the theory of categorical propositions. It bears on the analysis of conditional propositions, on the treatment of non-existent subjects, on the semantics of impossibilia, and on the structure of indirect proof. The essentialist reading, in particular, addresses a fundamental problem in the logic of universal quantification: how to make true claims about relations between essences or quiddities while circumventing the restrictions of existential import—and how, at the same time, to account for factual contingent truths.

*The referential and the descriptional reading.* While the essentialist/externalist distinction captures two different ways in which the subject term may be considered when evaluating a proposition, the second signature distinction in Rāzī's system captures two different ways in which the predicate holds (or does not hold) of that of which it holds (or does not hold). This is the distinction between the referential (or substantial) (*dhātī*) reading and the descriptional (*waṣfī*) reading of categorical propositions (2.1.1.6.6.1, pp. 150–152), introduced in the context of the analysis of necessity propositions and then applied, by extension, to other types of propositions. On the referential reading, a proposition expresses the relation between a predicate and the object(s) that the subject term designates, relative to the time of the continued existence of the object(s) and regardless of the way in which the subject term refers to the object(s) (e.g., at some time, at all times, necessarily, or otherwise). On the descriptional reading, a proposition expresses the relation between a predicate and the object(s) that the subject term designates *under the description* of the subject term, relative to the time(s) at which the subject term description applies. (The two temporal frames may happen to coincide, but they are conceptually distinct.) 

Thus, "Necessarily, every human is an animal" is a referential necessity: the predicate holds of the objects designated by "human" throughout the time of their existence. "Necessarily, everyone writing moves his hand" is a descriptional necessity: the predicate necessarily holds as long as "writing" is true of the objects of which it is true, but not (or, at least, not necessarily) throughout the time of their existence.

This distinction has important ramifications throughout Rāzī's system. Every categorical proposition in the *Mulakhkhaṣ* is implicitly classifiable as (i) a referential (*dhātī*), (ii) a descriptional (*waṣfī*), (iii) a conjunction of a descriptional and a referential component, or (iv) a conjunction of two referential components. This fourfold classification provides the basis for the canonical taxonomy of propositions that follows.[18]

*The canonical list of thirteen categorical propositions.* In Avicenna's logic, the distinction between the referential and the descriptional reading serves as the basis for an elaborate account of different kinds of implicitly or explicitly modal propositions involving temporal and alethic modalities, or a combination of both. Rāzī introduces a list of thirteen (sometimes expanded to fifteen) types of categorical propositions (2.1.1.6.6, pp. 154–170).[19] Categorical propositions are divided first into simple and compound. Simple propositions are either referential or descriptional. Compound propositions are conjunctions of two categorical propositions: the first conjunct may be either referential or descriptional, while the second is always a referential proposition specifying a restriction on the time at which the subject term of the first conjunct applies to the objects it designates. Simple referential propositions are: (1) the general absolute proposition (*muṭlaqa ʿāmma*), which asserts that the predicate holds of the subject at least once during its existence; (2) the perpetuity proposition (*dāʾima*), which asserts that the predicate holds at all times during the subject's existence; (3) the necessity proposition (*ḍarūriyya*), which asserts that the predicate holds necessarily; and (4) the general (one-sided) possibility proposition (*mumkina ʿāmma*), which asserts that the predicate holds possibly. Simple descriptional propositions are: (5) the general conditioned proposition (*mashrūṭa ʿāmma*), which is the descriptional counterpart of necessity (this should not be confused with the hypothetical conditional proposition (*sharṭiyya muttaṣila*), which is a proper "if …, then …" statement of an altogether different logical type); and (6) the general conventional proposition (*ʿurfiyya ʿāmma*), the descriptional counterpart of perpetuity. Compound propositions—each consisting of a primary assertion conjoined with a restrictive clause– include: (7) the non-necessary existential (*wujūdiyya lā ḍarūriyya*), (8) the non-perpetual existential (*wujūdiyya lā dāʾima*), (9) the temporal (*waqtiyya*), (10) the indeterminate (*muntashira*), (11) the special possibility (*mumkina khāṣṣa*), (12) the special conditioned (*mashrūṭa khāṣṣa*), and (13) the special conventional (*ʿurfiyya khāṣṣa*). Compound propositions thus qualify the first conjunct by a restriction that limits the temporal or modal scope of the first conjunct: for instance, the non-necessary existential affirmative universal asserts that the predicate holds at least once but not necessarily of all of the objects to which the subject term refers during the time of their continued existence—the conjunction of an existential affirmation with the negation of necessity.[20]

While the conceptual ingredients of this taxonomy are largely present in Avicenna, it is Rāzī who is responsible for regimenting the different types and stabilizing the vocabulary. Someone who were to pick up a logical text written in Arabic after the thirteenth century would be hard pressed to find technical vocabulary that does not ultimately trace back—directly or indirectly—to formulations first crystallized in the *Mulakhkhaṣ*.[21]

*Contradiction, conversion, and contraposition.* Such a complex tableau of propositions requires a correspondingly sophisticated theory of three logical operations: contradiction (*tanāquḍ*, 2.1.1.6.9.1, pp. 175–190), conversion (*ʿaks mustawin*, 2.1.1.6.9.2, pp. 190–200), and contraposition (*ʿaks an-naqīḍ*, 2.1.1.6.9.3, pp. 200–204). The treatment of contradiction opens with a definition and a reduction of the conditions for the unity of the opposing propositions (eight traditional conditions are reduced by Rāzī to three), followed by a systematic enumeration of the contradictories of all thirteen proposition types. The theory of contradiction for compound propositions is especially intricate, since a compound proposition is a conjunction of two sub-propositions, and the truth conditions of its contradictory are accordingly disjunctive. This means that for each compound proposition type, multiple disjunctive sub-cases must be tracked. A perpetuity proposition conjoined with a non-necessity restriction, for example, may be contradicted disjunctively by either the contradictory of the perpetuity component or the contradictory of the restriction—each of which must itself be identified within the taxonomy (compound descriptional in turn are typically contradicted by three-fold conditions).

The treatment of conversion is of particular importance for the syllogistic, since many proofs of productivity in the second and third figures depend on conversion. Rāzī includes an influential formulation of a general meta-logical principle: "If the stronger does not convert, then the weaker does not convert" (2.1.1.6.9.2, p. 197)—a rule of considerable heuristic and systematic utility for navigating the large number of conversion results across the thirteen types.[22] The results of conversion are given for all modalized proposition types, with separate treatment for negative and affirmative propositions on both the essentialist and the externalist reading of the subject term.

**Hypothetical propositions.** The treatment of hypothetical propositions (pp. 205–237) follows the Avicennan framework in its fundamental structure. Hypothetical propositions are divided into conditionals (*muttaṣilāt*) and disjunctives (*munfaṣilāt*). Among conditionals, Rāzī maintains the Avicennan distinction between the coincidental (*ittifāqī*) and the implicative (*luzūmī*).[23] Among disjunctives, he distinguishes three types: the real disjunction (*ḥaqīqiyya*), which is both exhaustive and exclusive—requiring exactly one disjunct to be true; the exclusive disjunction (*māniʿ al-jamʿ*), which rules out joint truth but allows joint falsehood; and the inclusive disjunction (*māniʿ al-khuluww*), which rules out joint falsehood but allows joint truth. The real disjunction is thus equivalent to the conjunction of the other two.[24]

Rāzī's treatment includes detailed discussions of the composition of hypothetical propositions (2.1.2.3, pp. 215–220). A conditional proposition has an antecedent and a consequent, each of which may be categorical, conditional, or disjunctive; this yields nine possible types of conditional proposition: (i) categorical-categorical, (ii) conditional-conditional, (iii) disjunctive-disjunctive, (iv) categorical-conditional, (v) conditional-categorical, (vi) categorical-disjunctive, (vii) disjunctive-categorical, (viii) conditional-disjunctive, (ix) disjunctive-conditional (2.1.2.3.1, pp. 214-5). The situation is analogous for disjunctives, but since in this case the order of the two disjuncts is irrelevant, some of the above combinations, namely (iv)-(v), (vi)-(vii), and (viii)-(ix), are logically equivalent. There are consequently only six genuinely distinct types of disjunctive proposition. Further, the parts of hypothetical propositions may share terms entirely, partially, or not at all, and the position of the hypothetical particle relative to the subject generates further distinctions (2.1.2.4, pp. 217–220). Rāzī also maintains a distinctively original feature of Avicenna's theory of hypotheticals, namely quantification. The quantification of hypothetical propositions takes the conditional or disjunctive relation between the parts of a hypothetical proposition (antecedent and consequent in one case, the two disjuncts in the other) to be itself subject to affirmation or negation (2.1.2.5, pp. 221-222), and to hold at different times––always, never, sometimes, not always––and under different circumstances (2.1.2.7, pp. 223–228). The four quantified conditional propositions and the four quantified disjunctive propositions are introduced with their own specific sets of truth conditions. Rāzī adopts Avicenna's characterization of implicative conditional *a*-propositions (2.1.2.7.2.1, pp. 224–225) and  extends the essentialist/externalist distinction to the analysis of conditional propositions—a natural move, given that both the antecedent and the consequent of a conditional may themselves be categorical propositions subject to these readings. A discussion of the modes of hypothetical propositions (2.1.2.11, p. 235) and their respective rules of contradiction and conversion (2.1.2.12-13, pp. 236–237) round out the treatment.[25]

An extraordinary example of Rāzī's classificatory virtuosity is found in his analysis of the universal affirmative conditional proposition with categorical parts (2.1.2.8, pp. 229–231). In determining how many types of such propositions are possible, Rāzī systematically considers every relevant parameter: the quality and quantity of the categorical antecedent and consequent (yielding, for each, six types of subject and predicate, multiplied by four combinations of determinate and indeterminate terms, giving 24 basic types for each part); the bipartite or tripartite structure; the sharing of terms between antecedent and consequent; the truth values of the parts; and the modalization of the conditional itself (with fifteen types). The product of these parameters yields an extraordinary figure: 552,960 possible types. Rāzī notes that Avicenna's classification envisages only sixteen types, being limited to the quality and quantity of antecedent and consequent. Rāzī does not propose to enumerate all 552,960; the point is to display the combinatorial richness hidden within a single logical form—a display that is at once laborious and illuminating, and that epitomizes Rāzī's distinctive approach to the discipline.

### The Syllogistic

The treatment of the categorical and hypothetical syllogistic (2.2, pp. 239–330) occupies the second section of the second *jumla*. After preliminary discussions of the divisions of proof (*ḥujja*), the definition of the syllogism, and the division of syllogisms into connective (*iqtirānī*) and repetitive (*istithnāʾī*) (2.2.1, pp. 241–251), Rāzī turns to the productive syllogisms themselves, organized into four main divisions (*aqsām*): (i) simple categorical syllogisms, (ii) modally mixed categorical syllogisms, (iii) hypothetical syllogisms (which jointly constitute the class of connective syllogisms), and (iv) repetitive syllogisms.

**Simple categorical syllogisms.** Simple categorical syllogisms from absolute premises are treated by figure: the first figure (2.2.2.1.1, pp. 253–259), the second (2.2.2.1.2, pp. 259–262), the third (2.2.2.1.3, pp. 263–265), and the fourth (2.2.2.1.4, pp. 265–271). The acknowledgement of the independent status of the fourth figure is one of Rāzī's most conspicuous expansions of the Avicennan system. Avicenna had rejected the fourth figure as a spurious innovation of late ancient logic and did not recognize it as a distinct class of productive inference schemas; Rāzī reverses this verdict and provides a systematic treatment of its productive moods. The addition would remain a point of discussion and occasional controversy in the later tradition.[26]

Rāzī's analysis of syllogisms in the first three figures is largely dependent on Avicenna' treatment of the topic in *Ishārāt*: it proceeds figure by figure rather than premise pair by premise pair and broadly follows, albeit with signfiicant oscillations, the rule of the major (the modality of the conclusion aligns with that of the major premise). For each figure, Rāzī identifies the productive moods—recognizing the four traditional inference schemas in the first figure (*Barbara*, *Celarent*, *Darii*, *Ferio*—or, in the technical notation, 1-aaa, 1-eae, 1-aii, 1-eio) as well as certain additional indirect moods (such as 1-aai and 1-aeo).[27] The first figure is treated at greatest length and includes a section of doubts and objections (*shukūk*) on the first mood (2.2.2.1.1, pp. 257–259), followed by their resolution—a characteristic Rāzian feature that echoes the dialectical structure of his theological works.

**Modally mixed categorical syllogisms.** The treatment of modally mixed syllogisms (2.2.2.2, pp. 272–306) represents the largest and most technically demanding portion of the syllogistic, and it is here that the full combinatorial power of the taxonomy of the thirteen propositions is deployed. The minor premise and the major premise can each be any one of the thirteen categorical propositions: the resulting combinations amount to one hundred and sixty nine. Not all of these are productive. Before enumerating the productive pairs, Rāzī identifies groups of necessary conditions that allow him to eliminate non-productive combinations systematically. A noteworthy preliminary condition establishes, for example, that when the minor premise is a possibility proposition, the pair is productive only when the major premise is a necessity, perpetuity, non-necessary existential, non-perpetual existential, or general possibility proposition. These preliminary filters are a distinctive methodological feature of Rāzī's approach: they allow the reader to navigate the vast logical space efficiently, rather than proceeding through each of the one hundred and sixty nine cases individually.

The productive premise pairs for the first figure are then enumerated (beginning at p. 279), and for each productive pair, Rāzī determines the modality of the conclusion. For each type of conclusion, Rāzī recognizes in the first figure the four standard inference schemas, generating a large but finitely ordered set of valid moods. Thus, for instance, Rāzī accepts the validity of *Barbara* LML (a necessity major with a possibility minor yielding a necessity conclusion), *Ferio* LML, and a host of other modal combinations—yielding hundreds of productive moods when the standard four productive combinations of quality and quantity are taken into account across all productive modal premise pairs.[28] Perhaps the most noteworthy feature of Rāzī's modal syllogistic is his acceptance of inferences with a possibility minor premise as straightforwardly valid—a position that would become the subject of heated controversy in the later tradition and would be challenged by the two logicians most influenced by Rāzī, namely Khūnajī and Kātibī.

The second figure raises a classical problem in post-Avicennan modal syllogistic. Since the Aristotelian assertoric proposition has been replaced by the absolute proposition (which involves a temporal quantifier), the conversion of universal negative propositions does not hold without qualification. As a result, most modal premise pairs in the second figure fail to be productive. An important exception, already recognized by Avicenna and adopted by Rāzī, is that necessity and perpetuity propositions do convert in the way required for the productivity of second-figure moods—most proofs of which rely precisely on the conversion of the universal negative.[29] The third figure receives the shortest treatment; the productive moods here typically rely on the conversion of the minor premise.

The fourth figure, as noted above, is Rāzī's principal expansion of the inherited framework and a departure from Avicenna, who had explicitly rejected it. Rāzī himself offers an interesting remark at the end of his treatment of simple absolutes in the fourth figure, observing that "complicating the discourse on these absolutes is of no use whatsoever for the sciences" (2.2.2.1.4, p. 271)—a candid acknowledgment that the combinatorial elaboration of the system can reach a point of diminishing practical returns, even as the theoretical completeness of the account requires it.


**Hypothetical syllogisms.** The treatment of hypothetical syllogisms (2.2.2.3, pp. 306–327) is considerably shorter and more cursory than the categorical. Rāzī broadly follows the Avicennan division of the subject, again with the exception of the fourth figure. Hypothetical syllogisms are classified on the basis of their premises into two main classes. The first includes wholly hypothetical syllogisms (also called "connective," *iqtirānī* like their categorical counterparts), where both premises are hypothetical—both conditional, both disjunctive, or mixed. The second includes mixed hypothetical syllogisms, where one premise is hypothetical and the other categorical; further sub-divisions depend on which premise is the major and which the minor. Repetitive syllogisms (*qiyās istiṯnāʾī*)—corresponding broadly to propositional inference schemas including *modus ponens*, *modus tollens*, and disjunctive syllogism—are treated separately (2.2.2.5, pp. 320–327)[30], divided into cases with a conditional major and cases with a disjunctive major. The treatment is relatively abbreviated, but it is nonetheless important because Rāzī is committed, as is Avicenna, to an understanding of *reductio ad absurdum* (*qiyās al-khalf*) as a compound syllogism consisting of a connective syllogism and a repetitive syllogism.

### The Eclipse of Demonstration

The treatment of demonstration (*burhān*) occupies the final section of the second *jumla* (2.3, pp. 331–340)—a mere ten pages, in striking contrast to the extensive coverage devoted to the theory of propositions and the syllogistic. The section addresses four topics: the definition of demonstration, the principles of demonstration, doubts concerning the received account, and (perhaps oddly) fallacies.[31]

The brevity of this section is in fact its salient feature. In Avicenna's *Shifāʾ*, the *Burhān* is a full-length treatment of the subject, running to hundreds of pages and engaging systematically with the themes of Aristotle's *Posterior Analytics*: the conditions of demonstrative premises, the epistemic status of first principles, the role of causally explanatory middle terms, the distinction between demonstrations of the fact and demonstrations of the reason why, and the theory of definition required for demonstrative reasoning. In the *Ishārāt*, the coverage is already reduced but still includes some of the traditional distinctions and problems. As a matter of fact, in the *Mulakhkhaṣ*, it approaches what might be called a total eclipse (the same true, perhaps even more so, of the fallacies).[32] Rāzī opens with an expression of surprise: the earlier logicians talk at length about the subject (i.e., devote space and time to the treatment of the *Posterior Analytics*) but this is odd. For if demonstration is nothing other than valid reasoning applied to premises of certitude that consequently lead by necessity to conclusions of certitude, what is there to discuss other than what constitutes a premise of certitude (or scientific principle)? Aristotle's response was that there is a lot to discuss over and above the formal conditions of validity, for example, the nature of middle terms, how causality is expressed in demonstration and definition. In the new format, starting already in Avicenna, elements of the theory are disarticulated: the account of definition is paired with the treatment of the predicables and is somehow confined to the logic of conceptions. A standard Avicennan classification of the different types of principles of *taṣdīq* (premises), based on different epistemic and modal conditions governing the strength of our assertions, receives a dedicated treatment by Avicenna in path 6 of the *Ishārāt*, while in the *Shifāʾ*, it still squarely falls under the treatment of *An. Post.* (in *Burhān* I, 4). The little else that survives from *Burhān*–– confined, in a cursory form, to path 9 of the *Ishārāt*––is essentially obliterated by Rāzī in the *Mulakhkhaṣ*, which exclusively focuses on the classification of principles. Or it is rerouted, following Avicenna's *Ishārāt*, to the early discussion of the predicables in path 1. This includes, most notably, the analysis of different senses of per se (*dhātī*) attributes (constitutive and non-constitutive) and of different types of necessary implicates (*lawāzim*), which is part of an extended discussion in *Burhān* II, 2–3. Thus, if a partial eclipse no doubt began with the late Avicenna, it becomes total with Rāzī, with the further theoretical twist that according to Rāzī, the formal dimension of logic almost entirely absorbs all there is to say about demonstrative reasoning, which is above all just formal reasoning.

The reasons for this contraction are a matter of interpretation, and it is interesting to observe how Ibn Khaldūn's claim––that the discipline had been transformed beyond recognition––tracks the internal development we see in the *Mulakhkhaṣ*. One possibility is that Rāzī regards the substantive questions of demonstrative theory as having been settled by Avicenna, so that extended treatment is unnecessary. Another—perhaps more telling—is that the architecture of the *Mulakhkhaṣ* itself reflects a different conception of the relationship between formal logic and the theory of science. Consider the way logic is structured and characterized in the work: as a science, with a precise subject matter (itself one of the contested topics in post-Avicennan philosophy), the proofs of which are conducted with demonstrative rigor. The conditions of demonstrative knowledge are, in an important sense, distributed across the earlier sections of the work: the careful treatment of modality, the analysis of necessity and perpetuity propositions, the investigation of the conditions under which syllogisms are productive—all of this is the material out of which demonstrations are built. What Aristotle treats in the *Posterior Analytics* as an independent theory of the structure of scientific knowledge has, in the *Mulakhkhaṣ*, been effectively absorbed into the body of formal logic. Demonstration, on this view, becomes less a separate theoretical subject than a *logica utens*: a capacity exercised in and through the formal apparatus, rather than an additional body of doctrine requiring independent treatment. This also resolves a potential ambiguity in the claim that Rāzī focuses primarily on the formal as opposed to the material dimension of logic. There are three ways to understand the distinction between "formal" and "material" in the context of this logical tradition: in the sense (i) of abstracting from concrete terms, (ii) of considering the semantic determinacy of the relation  between terms and propositions, or (iii) of considering the epistemic assurance with which we can assert that relation. A theory of the matter of deductions in the last sense, which is still a significant concernd for Avicenna,  no longer needs to be articulated within logic itself according to Rāzī (at least judging from his choices and practice). The logic of assertion concerns truth conditions and deductive structures, and those are the sole theoretically relevant and challenging objects of inquiry. The question of how to *use* logic (in demonstration or dialectic) or how to *misuse* it (in sophistry) is separate from and, to an extent, less problematic than the question of how to identify and recognize truth conditions and deductive structures. Logic is concerned only with the latter, not with the former.

The eclipse of *burhān* in the *Mulakhkhaṣ* is, in this light, not necessarily a deficiency but a deliberate architectural choice—one that, whether or not it is philosophically defensible, would prove influential on the shape of the later logical tradition. The themes of the *Posterior Analytics*—the conditions of demonstrative premises, the epistemic classification of assertions, the relationship between demonstration and definition—do not vanish from Rāzī's logical horizon; they become, as it were, unseen, operative within the system rather than articulated as a separate subject. It is worth noting, in this connection, that the proofs conducted throughout the *Mulakhkhaṣ*—of the productivity of syllogistic moods, of conversion results, of the properties of contradiction—are themselves conducted with rigorous demonstrative ambitions, even though the theory of demonstration as a separate discipline has been curtailed.

## Conclusion

The logic of the *Mulakhkhaṣ* occupies a position in the history of Arabic logic that is at once pivotal and—despite the growing attention of recent scholarship—still insufficiently explored in its full scope and detail. Rāzī's achievement is multi-dimensional. As a systematizer, he imposes on the Avicennan legacy a degree of classificatory order that made it, for the first time, truly navigable as a coherent system. As a terminological architect, he stabilizes a technical vocabulary—the *dhātī* and *waṣfī*, *ḥaqīqī* and *khārijī* distinctions, the canonical thirteen propositions, the fourfold classification of compound modalized propositions—that would serve as the standard idiom of Arabic logic for centuries. As an innovator, he expands the system in significant respects (e.g., by reinstating the fourth figure and by offering a powerful combinatorial analysis of the number of hypothetical propositions) and contracts it in others (demonstration and fallacies), reshaping the discipline's internal proportions in ways that reflects a considered, if debatable, view of what matters most.

The influence of the *Mulakhkhaṣ* on the later tradition can hardly be overstated. Najm ad-Dīn al-Kātibī's *Shamsiyya*, one of the most widely studied logic textbooks in the Islamic world from the thirteenth century onward, is deeply dependent on Rāzī's formulations and classifications.[33] The commentary tradition that grew around these later handbooks would continue to engage with distinctions and problems first articulated—or first given stable form—in the *Mulakhkhaṣ*. To understand the Arabic logical tradition after the thirteenth century without reference to Rāzī would be a hopeless endeavour, not only because Rāzī is a key historical bridge between Avicenna and that tradition, but also becuse he conceptually translates Avicenna's logic and transforms it into a new canon open to commentary and further theoretical development. It is not definitively clear how committed Rāzī truly is to a single systematic vision of logic––as opposed to Avicenna. At times, Rāzī's interest in dialectical engagement seems to work against a single systematic vision. But the fact that he wrestles with these two approaches becomes in a way productive for the later tradition. To put on hold a single systematic vision opens the gate to the generation of questions on a fractal scale, in a way akin to what Rāzī famously does in the commentary on the *Fātiḥa*. 

Much remains to be done. A critical edition of the *Mulakhkhaṣ* informed by the full manuscript tradition is a desideratum. The relationship between the *Mulakhkhaṣ* and  *al-Manṭiq al-kabīr*—whatever the latter's authorship—deserves sustained study, as does the broader relationship between Rāzī's independent logical works and the logic deployed in his philosophical and theological writings. The reception of Rāzī's logical doctrines in specific later figures—Khūnajī, whose *Kashf al-asrār* engages systematically with the modal syllogistic; Kātibī, whose *Shamsiyya* became the dominant logic textbook of the later tradition for Urmawī, Taḥtānī, and Taftāzānī—has begun to attract scholarly attention but is far from fully mapped.[34] The thirteenth and fourteenth centuries represent what might be called the age of handbooks, in which the massive and sometimes unwieldy architectures of Avicenna and Rāzī were distilled into more compact pedagogical instruments; understanding how this process of distillation worked—what was preserved, what was simplified, and what was lost—is essential for a full appreciation of Rāzī's place in the history of the discipline. And the relationship between Rāzī's logical work and his broader philosophical and theological enterprise—including the deployment of logical tools in Koranic exegesis—remains a rich and largely untapped field of inquiry.[35]

The text nonetheless deserves careful study in the larger context of the *Wirkungsgeschichte* of Rāzī's logical views, not least because it aligns the contents of logic with the *Organon* and contains extensive sections on areas that are missing in the systematic presentation of the *Mulakhkhaṣ*, such as the counterparts of Aristotle's *Posterior Analytics* and *Topics* and their reinterpretation by Avicenna in the *Burhān* and *Jadal* sections of the *Shifāʾ*.
